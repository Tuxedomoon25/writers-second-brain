# Story Structure Reference

> Condensed craft reference for `/story-forge` and `/character-forge`. Synthesized from multiple story structure frameworks.

---

## 1. Three-Act Structure Overview

| Act | Purpose | Length | Key Question |
|-----|---------|--------|-------------|
| **Act 1** | Setup — establish character in Lie, push out of comfort zone | ~25% | "Who is this person, and why should I care?" |
| **Act 2** | Confrontation — test the Lie, escalate, break everything | ~50% | "How far will they go, and what will it cost?" |
| **Act 3** | Resolution — face the Truth, transform (or fall), resolve | ~25% | "Who have they become?" |

**The Engine:** Every story is powered by **internal conflict** (desire vs. fear). Plot events only matter insofar as they force the protagonist to confront their Lie.

**The Metaphor:** Plot is the fist. Internal conflict is the punch. Without the punch, the fist has no power.

## 2. The Beat Map

### Act 1 Beats

**Beat 1: THE HOOK (0-6%)**
- Establish the protagonist living in their comfort zone
- Show the Lie in operation — through behavior, not exposition
- Internal conflict (desire vs. fear) must be "boiling below the surface" BEFORE the inciting incident
- **Key question:** "What does my protagonist want, and what fear is stopping them?"

**Beat 2: THE INCITING INCIDENT (6-12%)**
- An external event that pushes the protagonist outside their comfort zone
- Must be specific to THIS character (not generically bad — specifically threatening to their Lie)
- Does NOT need to be epic — just needs to disrupt THIS person's world
- Leads directly to the "impossible choice"
- **Key question:** "Why does this event matter to my protagonist specifically?"

**Beat 3: THE FIRST PLOT POINT (12-25%)**
- The protagonist CHOOSES to step into the unknown (active decision, not passive victim)
- "Pain vs. pain" — staying is now more painful than risking change
- The decision is fear-based — they pursue the Want while trying to avoid the Fear
- This launches Act 2
- **Key question:** "Why does my character believe this is the less painful option?"

**Beat 4: FIRST PINCH POINT (End of Act 1)**
- A hint of the antagonistic force looming ahead
- Uses suspense (knowing the bomb is there), not surprise (bomb going off)
- Three methods: Vicarious (reader knows, character doesn't), Shared (both know), Subtle clues
- **Key question:** "How can I show the reader that trouble is coming?"

### Act 2 Beats

**Beat 5: THE REACTIONARY HERO (25-50%)**
- Protagonist executes the "Stupid Plan" — fear-based, built on the Lie
- Every decision grounded in the specific misbelief (not generic motivation)
- Create a chain of cause and effect — one decision leads to the next
- The plan seems smart to the hero but the reader senses it's doomed
- **Key question:** "What is their step-by-step plan, and why is it based on a false belief?"

**Beat 6: THE MIDPOINT (50%)**
- A game-changing event that upends everything
- Prioritize internal impact over shock value — it must shift the protagonist's GOAL
- Creates a "Point of No Return" — they cannot go back to who they were
- The Truth is glimpsed but not yet accepted
- **Key question:** "What event makes the old plan impossible and forces a new approach?"

**Beat 7: THE SECOND PINCH POINT (50-75%)**
- The antagonistic force becomes more pressing and personal
- Protagonist forms a NEW plan (still based on the Lie, still doomed)
- Forces an "impossible choice" that tightens the screws
- **Key question:** "How does the opposition become personal?"

**Beat 8: BOOM! DISASTER (75%)**
- Everything falls apart — the protagonist's SPECIFIC fear is realized
- The protagonist is TO BLAME (not random bad luck — their Lie caused this)
- The "Dark Moment" — they face what they've been avoiding all story
- Optional: precede with a "supposed victory" to make the fall harder
- **Key question:** "How is my protagonist responsible for their own disaster?"

### Act 3 Beats

**Beat 9: THE TEMPTATION (75-85%)**
- An opportunity to achieve the Want without embracing the Truth
- The "perfect" shortcut — everything they desired, handed to them
- They REFUSE — proving transformation has begun
- **Key question:** "What would tempt them to go back to the Lie?"

**Beat 10: THE AHA MOMENT (85%)**
- The most important beat in the story
- Internal revelation — the protagonist understands the Truth (head AND heart)
- Must include the author's personal "why" — what truth are YOU trying to express?
- Triggers the transformation that powers the climax
- **Key question:** "What does my character realize about themselves AND about how they've hurt others?"

**Beat 11: THE CLIMAX (85-95%)**
- Transformation proven through ACTION — they DO something that costs them
- The Want and the Need are in direct conflict — they cannot have both
- They sacrifice the Want to embrace the Need
- Victory is meaningful, not just exciting
- **Key question:** "How does my character prove they've changed through what they DO, not what they say?"

**Beat 12: THE RESOLUTION (95-100%)**
- The Final Image mirrors the Opening Image but shows the change
- Same situation, different response — the Lie is gone
- Tie up subplots and relationship arcs
- **Key question:** "How does the ending mirror the beginning?"

## 3. Character Arc Mechanics — The Lie/Truth Engine

### The Core Components

| Component | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **The Lie (Misbelief)** | A false belief about self or world | "I am only worthy if I am successful" |
| **The Truth (Theme)** | What the story teaches | "Worth comes from who you are, not what you achieve" |
| **The Ghost** | The wound that created the Lie | The night his father called him worthless |
| **The Want** | External goal driven by the Lie | Become the most celebrated showman |
| **The Need** | Internal transformation = the Truth | Accept himself and his family as enough |
| **The Fear** | What they avoid (protects the Lie) | Being seen as ordinary, a nobody |
| **Comfort Zone** | Where the Lie feels safe | The routine, the known world |

### How Internal Drives External

```
Ghost → creates Lie → generates Fear → defines Comfort Zone
                ↓
    Inciting Incident disrupts Comfort Zone
                ↓
    Want (driven by Lie) → creates "Stupid Plan"
                ↓
    Plan fails because Lie is false
                ↓
    Disaster forces confrontation with Fear
                ↓
    Aha Moment: Truth understood
                ↓
    Climax: Need embraced through action (Want sacrificed)
```

### Building the Misbelief

1. **Identify the Truth** (your theme) → Flip it to find the Lie
2. **Locate the origin** → Write the Ghost scene (specific moment, not summary)
3. **Find the Cementing Moment** → A later event that confirmed the Lie
4. **Define current dissatisfaction** → How the Lie creates daily friction
5. **Set the false goal** → What the Lie tells them will fix everything
6. **Let the Lie drive the plot** → Every decision flows from the misbelief

## 4. Theme Integration

Theme is not a message you preach — it's a **question you explore through character behavior**.

| Method | How It Works |
|--------|-------------|
| **Lie = Anti-theme** | Act 1: protagonist lives the anti-theme through behavior |
| **Patterns of behavior** | Acts 1-2: show habits, choices, and relationships shaped by the Lie |
| **B-Story character** | The person who embodies the Truth — teaches through living, not lecturing |
| **Aha Moment = Theme stated** | Act 3: protagonist understands the Truth — the theme crystallizes |
| **Climax = Theme proven** | The protagonist acts on the Truth at personal cost |

**Rule:** Theme shows through what characters DO, never through what they SAY.

## 5. Villain / Negative Change Arc

The 5-beat tragic arc:

| Beat | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Conflicted Starting Point** | The villain-to-be has a wound AND a glimpse of truth. They're sympathetic. |
| **2. First Fear-Based Choice** | They choose the Lie over the Truth — out of fear, not malice. |
| **3. Game-Changing Midpoint** | Something happens that could redeem them — but they double down on the Lie. |
| **4. Acceptance of the Lie** | They fully commit. The Truth is no longer an option. This is where sympathy dies. |
| **5. The Tragic End** | The Lie consumes them. They get what they Wanted but lose what they Needed. |

**Key principle:** The best villains believe they are right. Their logic must be compelling enough that the reader thinks "I see why they believe that."

**Dark mirror:** The antagonist ideally wants the SAME thing as the protagonist but pursues it through different values. They're the version of the hero who made a different choice at the crossroads.

## 6. Scene/Sequel Pattern

Every scene in fiction follows one of two patterns:

### Scene (Action)
```
Goal     → What the POV character wants in this scene
Conflict → What opposes them (must escalate)
Disaster → They don't get what they want (or get it at unexpected cost)
```

### Sequel (Reaction)
```
Reaction → Emotional response to the disaster
Dilemma  → Two bad options (best bad choice)
Decision → Active choice → becomes the Goal of the next Scene
```

**Pacing rule:** More Sequels in early story (character building). Fewer Sequels toward climax (acceleration). Pure Scene at the climax.

### Five Commandments (for critical scenes)

For the 5 most important scenes (Inciting Incident, Midpoint, Disaster, Aha, Climax), verify:
1. Does the scene have its own inciting incident?
2. Do complications escalate to a turning point?
3. Is there a genuine crisis (best bad choice or irreconcilable goods)?
4. Does the character take ACTION?
5. Is there a clear resolution/new status quo?

## 7. Subplot Framework

### Purpose of Subplots

Subplots are not B-plots running alongside the main story — they are **mirrors and contrasts** that deepen the theme.

### The B-Story (Theme Carrier)

- The B-Story character teaches the protagonist what they need for the climax
- They embody the Truth through how they live, not through speeches
- The B-Story relationship often develops in "the upside-down world" of Act 2

### Subplot Design Rules

1. Every subplot must connect to the protagonist's Lie/Truth arc
2. Subplot characters answer the story's central question DIFFERENTLY than the protagonist
3. Subplots can mirror (same Lie, different outcome) or contrast (opposite Lie) the main arc
4. Resolve subplots slightly before the main climax — clear the stage

### The Argument Web (Truby)

Your cast is a web of arguments about the story's central question:

| Character | Their Answer | How They Test the Protagonist |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------------|
| Protagonist | [The Lie → The Truth] | — |
| Antagonist | [The Lie taken to extreme] | Forces confrontation |
| B-Story | [The Truth, lived quietly] | Teaches by example |
| Mirror | [Same Lie, further along] | Shows the cost |
| Contrast | [Opposite approach] | Shows the alternative |

## 8. Series Structure (Multi-Book)

For trilogies:
- **Book 1 = Act I** (Inception) — Establish the Lie, push into the macro conflict
- **Book 2 = Act II** (Journey) — Test, escalate, break at macro midpoint
- **Book 3 = Act III** (Battle) — Macro climax, Truth embodied at the highest scale

Each book has its own complete 3-act structure WITHIN the macro arc.

For 5-book series: Books 1-2 = Act I, Book 3 = Midpoint, Books 4-5 = Act III.
