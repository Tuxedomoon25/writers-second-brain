# Show Don't Tell — Quality Level Examples

The same moment at three quality levels: **A character receives news that someone they love has died.**

---

## Level 6/10 — Adequate

> Sarah felt devastated when she heard the news. She was overwhelmed with sadness and couldn't believe what had happened. The grief hit her hard and she realized nothing would ever be the same again. She felt tears forming in her eyes and knew she had to be strong, but it was difficult.

**Why 6/10:** Emotions are named directly ("devastated", "sadness", "grief"). The reader is told what to feel rather than shown it through physical action. "Realized" and "knew" are internal tells.

---

## Level 8/10 — Good

> Sarah set the phone on the counter. The screen stayed lit, the call timer still counting. She pressed both palms flat against the cold granite and focused on that — the temperature of the stone, the grain of it under her fingertips. The kettle behind her clicked off. Steam curled toward the ceiling. She watched it rise until her vision blurred, and then she was sliding, back against the cabinet, until the tile floor pressed cold through her jeans.

**Why 8/10:** Emotion is shown entirely through physical action and sensory detail. The reader infers grief from the behavior. Specific details (granite, kettle, tile floor) ground the scene. The only weakness: "her vision blurred" is slightly telling — tears implied but not quite earned through action.

---

## Level 10/10 — Excellent

> Sarah set the phone on the counter, screen-down. Her hand stayed there, covering it, as if the words might crawl back out. The kitchen was doing what kitchens do — the kettle ticking as it cooled, the tap dripping its patient morse code onto a forgotten spoon. She reached for the dishcloth and began wiping the counter in slow circles. The same spot. The same circle. The cloth was dry. The counter was clean. She kept wiping.

**Why 10/10:** Not a single emotion is named. The reader FEELS the numbness and denial through the repetitive wiping of a clean counter. The kitchen "doing what kitchens do" creates devastating contrast — the world carries on while hers has stopped. Every physical detail earns its place. The short final sentence ("She kept wiping.") lands like a punch.
