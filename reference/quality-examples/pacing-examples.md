# Pacing & Tension — Quality Level Examples

The same scene: **A character discovers they're being followed while walking home at night.**

---

## Level 6/10 — Adequate

> Marcus walked down the street and noticed that someone was following him. He felt nervous and started walking faster. The person behind him also walked faster. Marcus looked back and saw a figure in a dark coat. His heart was pounding and he wondered what to do. He decided to turn down a side street to see if the person would follow. The person did follow. Now Marcus was really scared and started running.

**Why 6/10:** Monotonous rhythm — every sentence is roughly the same length and structure (Subject + verb + object). No escalation in prose form. Emotions are told ("felt nervous", "really scared"). The pacing is flat despite the content being tense.

---

## Level 8/10 — Good

> Marcus first noticed the footsteps at the corner of Elm and Fourth. Steady. Matching his pace. He slowed — they slowed. He crossed the street — they crossed behind him, thirty seconds later.

> The rational part of his brain offered explanations. Same direction. Coincidence. The city had eight million people and some of them walked the same routes.

> He turned left on Maple. Not his usual way home.

> The footsteps turned left.

> Marcus pulled out his phone. Not to call anyone — just to hold something solid, something with a screen that pushed back the dark. His thumb hovered over the keypad. Three blocks to his apartment. Three blocks was nothing. Three blocks was a lifetime.

**Why 8/10:** Varied paragraph length creates rhythm — long analytical paragraph between short, punchy ones. The phone detail is specific and revealing. "Three blocks was nothing. Three blocks was a lifetime." uses repetition for escalation. Good tension, but the "rational part of his brain" paragraph slightly breaks the mounting dread.

---

## Level 10/10 — Excellent

> Elm and Fourth. Footsteps behind him.

> Marcus adjusted his grip on his bag and kept walking. People walked behind other people. That was how streets worked.

> He crossed at the light. Counted to ten. The footsteps crossed.

> Okay.

> He turned onto Maple — not his street, not his route, no reason for anyone to follow him onto Maple unless they were following him onto Maple. The footsteps turned.

> His phone was in his hand. He didn't remember taking it out. The screen threw pale light on the pavement and he watched his shoes move through it, left right left right, faster now, and behind him left right left right, faster now, and the gap between the sounds was closing, or maybe that was his imagination, or maybe it wasn't, and his building was two blocks away, two blocks, and his key was in his pocket, the one with the sharp edge, the one that always scratched his thigh, and he closed his fist around it and felt the metal bite into his palm and that was good, that was something, that was his.

**Why 10/10:** The pacing mirrors the experience. Short fragments at the start ("Elm and Fourth. Footsteps behind him.") establish unease. The middle tightens with logic breaking down. The final paragraph is one escalating run-on sentence — the prose itself breathless, matching Marcus's panic. The key detail transforms from mundane annoyance ("always scratched his thigh") into desperate weapon ("felt the metal bite into his palm"). Paragraph length shrinks as tension rises. "Okay." alone on a line is devastating.
