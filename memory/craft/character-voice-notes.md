# Character Voice — Craft Notes

> Insights about writing distinct character voices from 15 scenes. Updated 2026-03-20.

## Voice Differentiation Method

Character identity lives in **sentence rhythm and vocabulary register**, not accent or dialect. Each character has a measurable speech pattern:

### Sam (The Engineer)

- **Sentence type**: Fragmented, self-correcting, declarative
- **Vocabulary**: Technical — displacement, conservation, inverse square law, C4, L4 vertebra
- **Rhythm**: Staccato when stressed, clinical when explaining
- **Signature**: Dialogue sounds like code. Fragments → calculation → justification.
- **Example**: "Displacement. Block the point. Redirect the flow. Basic fluid dynamics."
- **Internal voice**: Italic fragments, 1-3 words, always diagnostic. "*Overheating.*" "*A parabola.*"
- **What he notices first**: The measurement. The structural weakness. The variable that doesn't fit.

### Ward (The Guide)

- **Sentence type**: Short declaratives, commands, weary truth
- **Vocabulary**: Operational — stop, go, stay, hold. When he explains, it's flat theology: "The Mist eats *names*."
- **Rhythm**: Single-word commands. When he speaks in full sentences, the weight increases.
- **Signature**: "Boy." One word that carries disappointment, urgency, and reluctant care simultaneously.
- **What he notices first**: The threat. The exit. The structural integrity of the defence.

### Sox (The Scribe)

- **Sentence type**: Quick, dismissive, rapid-fire
- **Vocabulary**: Dark humour, obituary cadence, rhetorical questions
- **Rhythm**: Fast. Self-interrupting. The quip arrives before the thought finishes.
- **Signature**: "Business is booming." / "Building a coffin? Make it double-wide."
- **The shift**: When Sox drops the irony (rare), the voice underneath is specific, careful, almost reverent. This happens once in Ch5 Sc2: "Then it had better work." — no quip, no deflection.
- **What he notices first**: The absurd detail. The weak spot. The practical escape route.

### Maya (The Bard)

- **Sentence type**: Simple, untagged, present-tense feeling
- **Vocabulary**: Sensory — hums, vibrations, colours, the Ache. Conversational, breathless.
- **Rhythm**: Soft. Minimal punctuation. Her lines feel like forces, not speech.
- **Signature**: Untagged questions that land like physical blows. "Can you hear it?" / "Why are you so afraid of it being real?"
- **What she notices first**: The feeling. The beauty. The thing everyone else is ignoring.

## Key Learning: Vocabulary Registers Must Never Cross

Sam never uses Maya's sensory language. Maya never uses Sam's technical language. Ward never quips. Sox never commands. The registers are firewalled.

**Exception**: When a character is changing (integration moment), their vocabulary shifts. Sam naming Ward back to life in Ch8 will use *specific nouns* (Ward's register) delivered with *feeling* (Maya's register) — the Snake and Dove fusing in speech.

## Dialogue Tag Economy

From 15 scenes of scoring:
- **"Said"**: 80% of tags. Invisible. Never draws attention.
- **Action beats**: Replace tags wherever possible. "She turned. 'Can you hear it?'"
- **No tag**: Used for Maya's force-lines and for rapid exchanges where rhythm > attribution.
- **Never used**: "exclaimed," "whispered" (except as physical action), "retorted," "mused"
