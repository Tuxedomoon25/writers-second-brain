# Dialogue Patterns

> Speech patterns, subtext techniques, and tag strategies extracted from 15 scenes. Updated 2026-03-20.

## HIGH Confidence

### 1. Tag Minimalism + Deferred Subtext

Dialogue carries factual or technical content. Emotional subtext lives in the *response gap* — the paragraph after the line, not the tag beside it. "Said" is the only invisible tag; action beats replace tags where possible.

- Guard: "Shadows have no weight. We do not open for wind." → Sam's response is pure logic: "The logic was circular... Argument would not break the loop." (Ch4 Sc2)
- Ward: "Sing, or step aside." / Sam: "I don't *sing*." / Ward: "Then step aside." Zero emotional scaffolding. (Ch5 Sc1)
- **Why it works**: Forces the reader to do the emotional math. When you don't explain what a line means, the reader feels it harder.

### 2. Character Voice via Rhythm, Not Dialect

Identity appears in sentence length, pacing, and vocabulary — never accent or grammar tricks.

- **Sam**: Technical, self-correcting, fragmented. "Displacement. Block the point. Redirect the flow. Basic fluid dynamics." Dialogue sounds like code — fragments, calculations, justifications.
- **Ward**: Short, declarative, tactical. "Boy." / "Stop." / "Go." Single-word commands. When he explains, the rhythm flattens into weary truth: "The Mist does not eat matter. The Mist eats *names*."
- **Sox**: Quick, dismissive, dark humour. Rapid-fire. "Business is booming." / "Building a coffin? Make it double-wide." Questions are rhetorical. Statements are final. When the irony drops (rare), the voice underneath is specific and careful.
- **Maya**: Presence, not performance. Minimal punctuation, often untagged. "Can you hear it?" / "It's more real than home." / "We are here. Please." Her statements land like forces, not speech.
- **Why it works**: Readers hear personality without explanation. The rhythm difference IS the character difference.

### 3. Love as Unspoken Variable

Maya is never narrated through Sam's emotional lens. His feelings exist only in what he DOESN'T say and what he DOES. Love is thermodynamic — it has mass and presence without requiring dialogue.

- "She weighed almost nothing... the mass of a girl who had been fading at the edges" (Ch4 Sc1)
- "He did not say *Maya*. He did not need to. The name sat in the room like heat — sourceless, present, specific enough to have weight." (Ch5 Sc2)
- "She looked at Sam the way she had looked at the Mystic Lamb — as though the world had offered evidence that beauty could also be a weapon." (Ch5 Sc3)
- **Why it works**: Sam's math bends around Maya. The reader feels love's weight because Sam can't calculate it.

## MEDIUM Confidence

### 4. Maya's Untagged Voice

Removing dialogue tags from Maya's lines makes her statements feel like environmental forces rather than speech. Used sparingly (5 moments across 15 scenes).

- "Why are you so afraid of it being real?" (Ch1 Sc2) — untagged, lands like a blow
- "It's more real than home." (Ch3 Sc2) — no attribution, pure statement
- **Why it works**: Maya's voice IS presence. Tags would domesticate it.

### 5. Sam's Italic Internal Fragments

Sam's internal voice appears as short, italicised diagnostic bursts. Never full sentences. Always compressed, clinical.

- "*Calibration error.*" (Ch3 Sc1)
- "*Overheating.*" (Ch5 Sc3 old draft — replaced by stomach-drop in v2)
- "*A parabola.*" (Ch5 Sc1 old draft)
- **Pattern**: 1-3 words. Technical register. No emotion. The brevity IS the emotion — Sam's Crown reducing catastrophe to a label.
- **Why it works**: Distinguishes internal from external voice. Creates comedy/pathos when the label is absurdly inadequate for the situation.
