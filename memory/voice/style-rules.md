# My Writing Rules — The Industrial Mystic

## Rule 1: Simple Past Tense
- **Rule**: Absolute. Non-negotiable. Creates "Lewisian Distance"
- **Why**: The narrator speaks from "the eternal after" — a legend told by firelight. Present tense breaks the mythic frame
- **Correct**: "She walked to the window. The glass held her reflection like a question."
- **Wrong**: "She walks to the window. The glass holds her reflection."

## Rule 2: Two-Layer Rule
- **Rule**: Layer 1 (Hemingway Anchor — physical fact) + Layer 2 (Lewis Echo — mythic meaning)
- **Why**: Every significant moment carries both body and soul. The physical grounds the mythic; the mythic elevates the physical
- **Correct**: "The watch ticked. It was the only honest sound in a city that had forgotten how to count."
- **Wrong**: "The watch ticked." (body only) or "Time itself was wounded." (soul only)

## Rule 3: Show Don't Tell (Iceberg Theory)
- **Rule**: Emotions shown as somatic truth, physical objects, or environmental reflection. Never named
- **Why**: Named emotions are instructions to feel. Shown emotions are invitations to recognise
- **Correct**: "His throat closed. The copper taste flooded his mouth." (= fear)
- **Wrong**: "He felt afraid." / "Fear gripped him."

## Rule 4: Verb Supremacy
- **Rule**: Zero -ly adverbs. Use strong, specific verbs instead
- **Why**: Adverbs are a tax on weak verbs. "Whispered" is stronger than "said quietly"
- **Correct**: "She crept through the doorway."
- **Wrong**: "She walked quietly through the doorway."

## Rule 5: Metaphor Limit
- **Rule**: One metaphor per paragraph maximum
- **Why**: Stacked metaphors cancel each other out. One metaphor given room to breathe is worth five competing for attention
- **Correct**: One metaphor, fully developed, then move on
- **Wrong**: "The fog was a curtain, a wall of silence, a blanket of grey erasure."

## Rule 6: Positive Assertions
- **Rule**: State what IS, not what isn't. Exception: Mist/Weaver/existential horror (where absence IS the point)
- **Why**: Positive assertions have more Mass (in the mythology's physics and in prose weight)
- **Correct**: "The corridor was dark." / "The stone held cold."
- **Wrong**: "It wasn't bright." / "There was no warmth."

## Rule 7: Anti-Abstraction Rule
- **Rule**: Magic described as solid physics — temperature, weight, texture, sound. Never as abstract concept
- **Why**: In The Sung World, the Song IS physics. Mass is Meaning. Magic has temperature, density, grain
- **Correct**: "The air thickened. The stone under his feet gained weight."
- **Wrong**: "A mystical energy filled the room." / "The ethereal force surrounded them."

## Rule 8: Tactile Anchor (The Knopfler Rule)
- **Rule**: Every scene needs a specific physical object that grounds the abstract in the concrete
- **Why**: One object carrying the scene gives the reader something to hold. Mark Knopfler's guitar does what a symphony cannot
- **Correct**: Sam's watch (400g, cracked face, ticking). The Weaver's needles. Ward's armour
- **Wrong**: Generic descriptions without a specific anchor object

## Rule 9: Formatting
- **Rule**: Em-dashes for pivots. Italics for emphasis. NO ALL-CAPS. No exclamation marks in narrative
- **Why**: Formatting is invisible craft. Em-dashes create the pivot the reader feels but doesn't see
- **Correct**: "The bell swung — and silence."
- **Wrong**: "The bell swung... and SILENCE!"

## Rule 10: Paragraph Wave Rhythm
- **Rule**: Long poetic opening → medium → short hammer. The wave pattern within each paragraph cluster
- **Why**: Rhythm creates the physical sensation of reading. The short sentence at the end of a long passage hits like a bell
- **Correct**: A flowing, comma-heavy opening sentence. A medium sentence with more weight. Then the hammer.
- **Wrong**: All sentences the same length. Monotonous rhythm. No variation.
