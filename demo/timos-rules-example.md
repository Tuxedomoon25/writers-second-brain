# Example: Timo's 10 Non-Negotiable Rules

> This is ONE example of how a writer defines their commandments. Your rules will be different — they should reflect YOUR voice, YOUR influences, YOUR craft instincts. Use this as inspiration, not a template to copy.

## The Voice: "The Industrial Mystic"

**In one sentence:** "The eye of Hemingway meets the soul of C.S. Lewis."

**What this means:** Physical precision anchors every sentence (Hemingway). Mythic weight lives underneath (Lewis). The result reads like a legend told by firelight — grounded in the body, reaching toward something larger.

## The 10 Commandments

### 1. Simple Past Tense (Lewisian Distance)
- **Rule:** Simple past tense exclusively. No present tense narration. No past perfect except in dialogue.
- **Why:** Creates narrative distance — implies a story being told after the fact, like a chronicle.
- **Correct:** "She walked to the window and stared."
- **Wrong:** "She walks to the window and stares."

### 2. Two-Layer Rule (Body + Soul)
- **Rule:** Every significant scene moment has two layers — a physical/concrete anchor (Hemingway) and a mythic/archetypal resonance (Lewis).
- **Why:** Prose that is only physical is flat. Prose that is only mythic is abstract. The combination creates depth.
- **Correct:** "The bell cracked along its seam, and something in the air went quiet — not silent, but un-named."
- **Wrong:** "The bell's destruction represented the loss of meaning in the community."

### 3. Show Don't Tell (Iceberg Theory)
- **Rule:** Never name an emotion. Show it through the body — clenched jaw, held breath, fingers pressing into a palm. The reader infers the rest.
- **Why:** Named emotions are instructions. Shown emotions are experiences.
- **Correct:** "Her hands wouldn't stop shaking. She pressed them flat against her thighs."
- **Wrong:** "She was afraid."

### 4. Verb Supremacy
- **Rule:** Zero tolerance for -ly adverbs. If you need an adverb, the verb is wrong.
- **Why:** Strong verbs carry their own energy. Adverbs are crutches.
- **Correct:** "She strode across the square."
- **Wrong:** "She walked quickly across the square."

### 5. One Metaphor Per Paragraph
- **Rule:** Maximum one metaphor per paragraph. Let it breathe. No stacking.
- **Why:** Multiple metaphors in a paragraph compete for attention and cancel each other out.

### 6. Positive Assertions
- **Rule:** Say what IS, not what isn't. Describe what the character sees, not what's absent.
- **Why:** Positive assertions are concrete. Negative assertions are abstract.
- **Correct:** "The room was silent."
- **Wrong:** "There was no sound in the room."

### 7. Anti-Abstraction (Magic as Physics)
- **Rule:** Magical or mythic elements described as physical phenomena, not abstract concepts.
- **Why:** Grounding the supernatural in the senses makes it feel real.
- **Correct:** "The Mist pressed against the windowpane like breath on cold glass."
- **Wrong:** "An evil presence filled the room."

### 8. Tactile Anchor (The Knopfler Rule)
- **Rule:** Every scene has a specific physical object that the character interacts with — a tactile anchor that grounds the scene.
- **Why:** Named after Mark Knopfler's guitar — one instrument that carries the whole song. One object that carries the whole scene.
- **Example:** A tactical watch. A cracked compass. A brass bell.

### 9. Formatting Rules
- **Em dashes** for interruptions and asides
- **Italics** for internal thought (sparingly)
- **No ALL CAPS** — ever
- **No exclamation marks** in narration

### 10. Paragraph Wave Rhythm
- **Rule:** Vary paragraph length — long, medium, short. End scenes on short sentences.
- **Why:** Creates a wave pattern that moves the reader forward. Short sentences after long ones hit like punches.

## What Makes This System TIMO's

These rules come from his specific influences (Hemingway + Lewis), his genre (literary fantasy with mythic undertones), and his craft instincts (precision over poetry, restraint over decoration).

**Your rules will be different.** A romance writer might allow more emotional language. A thriller writer might use present tense. A children's book author might embrace simple, warm descriptions.

The system doesn't care WHAT your rules are — only that you HAVE them and they're enforced consistently.
